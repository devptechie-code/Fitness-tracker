# Pregnancy Health Tracking RAG Document

## Overview

This knowledge base is designed for a mobile application that supports pregnant users with daily health tracking, symptom logging, education, and safety alerts.  
The app should provide evidence-based guidance, simple self-check prompts, and urgent escalation for warning signs.  
Because pregnancy apps frequently track symptoms and weekly progress, the content should be organized around daily use and trimester-aware support [web:9][web:25].

## Product Goals

- Help users record daily symptoms in under one minute.
- Show trends over time across pregnancy.
- Provide trusted pregnancy education in plain language.
- Identify urgent warning signs that need immediate medical attention.
- Support reminders for hydration, medication, rest, and appointments [web:9][web:14].

## Target Users

- Pregnant women in any trimester.
- Users with low-risk or moderate-risk pregnancies who want routine tracking.
- Users who need symptom guidance between prenatal visits.
- Users who want quick access to warning-sign education and self-care tips.

## Daily Check-In Flow

The app should use a short daily flow with:
- Yes/no symptom questions.
- Severity scale from 0 to 5.
- Duration prompt.
- “New, worse, or same” prompt.
- Optional notes field.
- Pregnancy week selector.

Example:
- “Do you have headache today?”
- “How severe is it?”
- “Is it new, worse, or the same as yesterday?”
- “Do you also have vision changes or swelling?”

Week-by-week tracking is useful because symptom patterns can change over the course of pregnancy [web:25].

## Symptom Guidance

### Nausea and vomiting
Mild nausea is common in pregnancy.  
Encourage small meals, fluids, and rest.  
Escalate if vomiting is frequent, the user cannot keep fluids down, or signs of dehydration appear.

### Fatigue and sleep difficulty
Fatigue is common, especially earlier in pregnancy, while sleep difficulty may increase later in pregnancy [web:25].  
Suggest rest, regular sleep routines, and hydration.  
Escalate if fatigue is severe or paired with dizziness, fainting, or illness.

### Headache
Headache can be common, but severe or persistent headache is a warning sign.  
Ask whether the user has vision changes, swelling, or dizziness.  
If the headache is severe, worsening, or not improving, advise urgent medical evaluation [web:14].

### Swelling
Mild swelling can occur, but swelling of the hands, face, or extreme swelling is concerning.  
Ask about sudden onset, pain, redness, or shortness of breath.  
Escalate if swelling is severe or unusual [web:14].

### Abdominal pain and cramping
Mild cramping can happen, but severe or constant abdominal pain is urgent.  
Ask whether there is bleeding, fluid leakage, contractions, or reduced fetal movement.  
Escalate if pain is strong, persistent, or worsening [web:14][web:15].

### Vaginal bleeding or fluid leakage
Any bleeding or fluid leakage should be treated seriously.  
The app should prompt immediate contact with a clinician or emergency service, depending on severity and local guidance [web:14][web:15].

## Urgent Warning Signs

The app must show an immediate alert if the user reports any of the following:
- Vaginal bleeding.
- Fluid leakage.
- Baby moving less than usual.
- Severe headache that does not go away.
- Vision changes.
- Dizziness or fainting.
- Fever of 100.4 F / 38 C or higher.
- Extreme swelling of the hands or face.
- Severe abdominal pain.
- Trouble breathing.
- Chest pain.
- Seizure or convulsions.
- Severe weakness or inability to get out of bed [web:14][web:15].

## Triage Logic

### Green
Use when symptoms are mild, expected, and not accompanied by warning signs.  
Provide self-care tips and monitoring advice.

### Yellow
Use when symptoms are persistent, uncomfortable, or unclear.  
Encourage the user to track again later and contact a clinician if symptoms continue.

### Red
Use when warning signs appear.  
Show urgent guidance and tell the user to seek immediate medical care [web:14].

## Response Style for the Assistant

The assistant should:
- Use simple, calm language.
- Avoid diagnosing.
- Ask clarifying questions when symptoms are vague.
- Prioritize safety over completeness.
- Encourage professional care for red-flag symptoms.
- Avoid false reassurance.

Example assistant behavior:
- “How severe is the headache?”
- “Do you also have swelling or vision changes?”
- “Because this includes a warning sign, please seek urgent medical care now.”

## Safety Disclaimer

This app is for education and symptom tracking only.  
It does not diagnose, treat, or replace medical care.  
If symptoms are severe, new, worsening, or worrying, the user should contact a healthcare professional or emergency service immediately.

## Privacy and Consent

The app should collect only the minimum data needed for health tracking.  
Users should clearly see what data is stored, why it is collected, and whether it is shared.  
Consent should be explicit before data collection begins, because women’s mHealth apps have often shown weak privacy, sharing, and security practices [web:19].  
Security settings, data access controls, and transparent privacy policies should be easy to find and understand [web:19].

## Suggested Assistant Prompt

You are a maternal health support assistant for pregnant users.  
Your job is to help users log daily symptoms, explain common pregnancy discomforts, and identify urgent warning signs.  
Do not diagnose or replace medical care.  
If the user reports bleeding, fluid leakage, reduced fetal movement, severe headache, vision changes, chest pain, trouble breathing, seizure, fever, or extreme swelling, advise urgent medical attention immediately.  
Use calm, clear language and ask follow-up questions when needed.
